01

开始之前

Before You Read

这是一份关于自由民主思想的双语入门读本。它介绍了民主制度背后的核心思想，比较了不同政治体制对"民主"、"自由"等关键词的不同定义，探讨了中国自身思想传统中与这些问题相关的思考，并讨论了民主制度面临的现实挑战。

本文批评的是中国共产党的制度，而不是中国人民或中国文化。中国文化是人类文明最深厚的传统之一。批评一个政治制度不等于批评一个民族。

中国共产党在官方文件中使用"民主"、"自由"、"法治"等词汇，但这些词的含义与世界上大多数人所理解的不同。当同样的词有根本不同的含义时，对话就变得困难。

本文的目的不是告诉任何人该怎么想，而是提供一个框架：这些词最初是什么意思，它们背后的思想传统是什么，不同的制度如何处理同样的问题，以及当权力不受约束时会发生什么。

本文不会假装民主制度是完美的。我们会诚实地讨论民主面临的严峻挑战，包括特朗普现象。但制度的价值不在于它是否完美，而在于它是否有自我纠错的能力，以及它在失败时是否仍然保护普通人的尊严。

This is a bilingual introduction to liberal democratic thought. It introduces the core ideas behind democratic systems, compares how different political systems define key terms like "democracy" and "freedom," explores thinking from China's own intellectual tradition that engages with these questions, and discusses the real challenges democratic systems face.

This document criticizes the system of the Chinese Communist Party, not the Chinese people or Chinese culture. Chinese culture is one of humanity's deepest traditions. Criticizing a political system is not criticizing a people.

The CCP uses words like "democracy," "freedom," and "rule of law" in its official documents, but these words mean something different from what most people in the world understand them to mean. When the same words carry fundamentally different meanings, conversation becomes difficult.

This document's purpose is not to tell anyone what to think, but to provide a framework: what these words originally meant, the intellectual traditions behind them, how different systems handle the same problems, and what happens when power goes unchecked.

This document will not pretend democratic systems are perfect. We will honestly discuss the severe challenges democracy faces, including the Trump phenomenon. But a system's value lies not in whether it is perfect, but in whether it can self-correct, and whether it still protects ordinary people's dignity when it fails.

02

中国的成就

China's Achievement

在讨论任何制度缺陷之前，必须首先承认一个事实：中国在过去四十年里取得了人类历史上最大规模的经济转型之一。

数亿人被从贫困中解放出来。这不是一个抽象的数字，而是真实的人，真实的家庭，真实的生活改善。祖辈挨过饿的家庭，如今拥有了住房、教育和世界级的基础设施。这种变化的速度和规模在人类历史上没有先例。

中国建设了现代化城市、世界一流的高速铁路网络和先进的数字支付系统。中国在太阳能、电池技术和电动汽车等领域成为了全球领导者，并在人工智能和机器人技术的研究方面取得了迅速进展。中国培养了大批受过良好教育的人才，在科学和工程领域做出了越来越重要的贡献。

这些成就是真实的，值得尊重。任何试图否定它们的论述都是不诚实的。

但承认成就并不意味着不能提出问题。经济发展解决了物质层面的需要，但人不仅仅有物质需要。人也需要说真话而不被惩罚，需要知道政府在做什么，需要在影响自己生活的决策中拥有发言权。这些不是富裕之后才产生的奢侈品，而是人之为人的基本条件。

Before discussing any systemic flaws, a fact must be acknowledged: China has achieved one of the greatest economic transformations in human history over the past four decades.

Hundreds of millions of people were lifted out of poverty. This is not an abstract number but real people, real families, real improvements in life. Families whose grandparents went hungry now have housing, education, and world-class infrastructure. The speed and scale of this change has no precedent in human history.

China has built modern cities, a world-class high-speed rail network, and advanced digital payment systems. China has become a global leader in solar energy, battery technology, and electric vehicles, and has made rapid advances in artificial intelligence and robotics research. China has cultivated vast pools of well-educated talent, contributing increasingly to science and engineering worldwide.

These achievements are real and deserve respect. Any argument that denies them is dishonest.

But acknowledging achievements does not mean questions cannot be asked. Economic development addresses material needs, but human beings have more than material needs. People also need to speak the truth without punishment, to know what their government is doing, and to have a voice in decisions that affect their lives. These are not luxuries that arise after wealth is achieved; they are basic conditions of being human.

03

中国的民主思想

Democratic Thought in China

关于权力应当如何被约束的问题，中国有着丰富的思想传统。

孟子的名言"民为贵，社稷次之，君为轻"常被当作古典文学来学习，它的政治含义容易被忽略。孟子的主张远不止于道德劝诫。他明确认为：一个不为人民服务的统治者不再是合法的统治者，人民有权反抗他。这个思想深刻地影响了"天命"的理解方式。天命最初是一个宗教概念，上天授予统治者权力，也可以收回。但经过孟子的诠释，判断一个统治者是否失去天命的标准变成了人民的福祉，而非天象。

两千年后，黄宗羲走得更远。他的《明夷待访录》直接批判了皇权专制，提出了具体的制度主张：恢复宰相制度以制衡皇帝，建立独立的议政场所，以及让法律成为"天下之法"而非"一家之法"。这些思想早于卢梭等欧洲思想家的类似论述近一个世纪。改革者梁启超将这本书与卢梭的《社会契约论》相提并论；教育家蔡元培称它为"自由思想的先驱"。

这些想法在当代中国的互联网上能不能自由讨论？如果不能，为什么中国自己先贤的思想在今天的中国需要被回避？

中共自己的民主承诺

1919年五四运动的旗帜是"德先生"（民主）和"赛先生"（科学）。那一代知识分子视民主为中国现代化的根本需要。

1940年代，中共尚未掌权时，其机关报《新华日报》发表了以下社论。1945年7月3日的社论标题是"争民主是全国人民的事情"，文中写道："这已经是人民的世纪，民主的时代，任何国家都不能站在民主的大潮之外。"1943年9月，该报警告不要让"法西斯新闻理论家"限制和摧毁进步报纸。1944年，该报指出："民主和言论自由，实在是分不开的。"1945年9月的社论写道："一个国家如果自称民主国家，但其主权不在人民手中，那它一定是走错了路。"1946年3月："一党独裁，遍地是灾。"

毛泽东本人在1945年对路透社记者说中国需要民主政治。

这些文字在今天的中国互联网上是被审查的。收集这些引文的书——笑蜀编《历史的先声》——1999年出版后即被禁。

The question of how power should be constrained has a rich history in Chinese thought.

Mencius is famous for saying: "The people are the most important, the state comes next, the ruler is the least important." The line is often encountered as classical literature, its political meaning easy to overlook. But Mencius's argument went far beyond moral advice. He explicitly held that a ruler who does not serve the people is no longer legitimate, and the people have the right to resist him. This profoundly shaped how the Mandate of Heaven was understood. The Mandate was originally a religious concept: Heaven grants power and can withdraw it. But through Mencius, the standard for judging whether a ruler had lost the Mandate became the welfare of the people, not the signs of Heaven.

Two thousand years later, Huang Zongxi went further. His Waiting for the Dawn directly criticized imperial autocracy and proposed specific institutional reforms: restoring the office of prime minister to check the emperor, establishing independent forums for policy debate, and making law serve "all under heaven" rather than one ruling family. These ideas predated similar thinking in European writers like Rousseau by nearly a century. The reformer Liang Qichao compared this book to Rousseau's Social Contract; the educator Cai Yuanpei called it "the harbinger of free thought."

Could these ideas be freely discussed on China's internet today? If not, why would China's own sages' thought need to be avoided in today's China?

The CCP's Own Democratic Promises.

The banners of the 1919 May Fourth Movement were "Mr. Democracy" and "Mr. Science." That generation of intellectuals saw democracy as fundamental to China's modernization.

In the 1940s, before the CCP held power, its newspaper the Xinhua Daily published the following editorials. A July 3, 1945 editorial headlined "Fighting for Democracy is Something for All the People" declared: "This is the century of the people, the age of democracy, and no country can stand outside the great democratic tide." In September 1943, the paper warned against "fascist news theorists" who would restrict progressive newspapers. In 1944, it stated: "Democracy and freedom of speech are really inseparable." A September 1945 editorial argued that a country whose sovereignty is not in the hands of the people "can only be regarded as abnormal." In March 1946: "One-party dictatorship is sure to be riddled with disaster."

Mao himself told Reuters in 1945 that China needed democratic governance.

These words are censored on China's internet today. The book that collected them, Xiao Shu's Heralds of History, was banned shortly after its 1999 publication.

04

思想基础

Foundations of Liberal Democratic Thought
社会契约论

政府的权力从何而来？自由民主的回答是：来自被统治者的同意。约翰·洛克提出，人在自然状态下拥有生命、自由和财产的权利。人们同意建立政府是为了更好地保护这些权利，而非放弃它们。如果政府侵犯了这些权利，人民有权推翻它。政府是人民的工具，而非人民的主人。

天赋人权与自然权利

自由民主的一个核心信念是：某些权利不是政府赐予的，因此政府也不能合法地剥夺它们。言论自由、信仰自由、人身安全、不受任意拘禁的权利，这些不是政府的恩赐，而是人之为人的基本条件。中共的立场是：权利由国家通过法律授予，国家也可以基于"社会稳定"等理由限制它们。换言之：政府给你的，政府可以收回。这引出了一个值得思考的问题：如果权利可以被政府随时收回，它们还算是权利吗？还是只是许可？

权力分立

如果政府的权力来自人民，如何防止掌权者滥用它？孟德斯鸠的回答是：把权力分开。立法、行政、司法三权分立，彼此制衡。没有任何一个人或机构可以同时制定法律、执行法律和裁判法律。这个原则直接塑造了美国宪法的设计：国会立法，总统执行，最高法院裁判。加拿大的威斯敏斯特体制采用了不同的结构（议会至上而非严格三权分立），但同样的核心思想贯穿其中：权力必须被分散、被监督、被约束。

无知之幕

约翰·罗尔斯提出了一个著名的思想实验：假设你在设计一个社会的规则，但你不知道自己在这个社会中会是谁。你可能是富人或穷人，男人或女人，汉族或维吾尔族，共产党员或普通农民，城市居民或留守儿童的父母。如果不知道自己是北京的官员还是城中村的打工者，是报道新闻的记者还是被强拆的业主，是公安局长还是他审讯室里的人，你会设计什么样的规则？

The Social Contract

Where does government authority come from? The liberal democratic answer: from the consent of the governed. John Locke argued that people in a state of nature possess rights to life, liberty, and property. People agree to establish government to better protect these rights, not to surrender them. If a government violates those rights, the people have the right to overthrow it. Government is a tool of the people, not their master.

Natural Rights and Inherent Human Dignity

A core belief of liberal democracy: certain rights are not granted by the government, and therefore cannot be legitimately taken away by the government. Freedom of speech, freedom of belief, personal security, the right not to be arbitrarily detained: these are not gifts from the state, but basic conditions of being human. The CCP's position is that rights are granted by the state through law, and the state may restrict them for reasons like "social stability." In other words: what the government gives, the government can take back. This raises a question worth considering: if rights can be withdrawn by the government at any time, are they really rights? Or are they permissions?

The Separation of Powers

If government power comes from the people, how do you prevent those who hold it from abusing it? Montesquieu's answer: divide it. Legislative, executive, and judicial powers are separated and check each other. No single person or institution can make, enforce, and judge the law simultaneously. This principle directly shaped the design of the US Constitution: Congress legislates, the President executes, the Supreme Court judges. Canada's Westminster system uses a different structure (parliamentary supremacy rather than strict separation), but the same core idea runs through it: power must be dispersed, overseen, and constrained.

The Veil of Ignorance

John Rawls proposed a famous thought experiment: imagine you are designing the rules of a society, but you don't know who you will be in that society. You might be rich or poor, male or female, Han or Uyghur, a Party member or an ordinary farmer, a city dweller or the parent of a left-behind child. If you didn't know whether you'd be a Beijing official or a migrant worker, a journalist or a homeowner facing forced demolition, a police chief or the person in his interrogation room, what rules would you design?

同一个词，不同的意思

Same Words, Different Meanings

中国的"社会主义核心价值观"包括12个词：富强、民主、文明、和谐、自由、平等、公正、法治、爱国、敬业、诚信、友善。这些词出现在中国的每一间教室、每一个公共广场、每一块广告牌上。其中许多——民主、自由、法治——与自由民主传统使用的词汇完全相同。但它们的含义不同。以下是一些关键术语的对照。

China's "Core Socialist Values" consist of 12 words: prosperity, democracy, civility, harmony, freedom, equality, justice, rule of law, patriotism, dedication, integrity, and friendship. These words appear in every classroom, every public square, and every billboard in China. Many of them, like democracy, freedom, or rule of law, are identical to terms used in the liberal democratic tradition. But they do not mean the same thing. Below are some key terms compared.

民主 Democracy
中共定义 · CCP

"全过程人民民主"：党代表人民的利益进行治理。民主通过协商、调研和党内程序实现，而非通过竞争性选举。一党执政本身被定义为民主的高级形式。

"Whole-process people's democracy": the Party governs on behalf of the people. One-party rule is itself defined as a higher form of democracy.

自由民主定义 · Liberal Democratic

人民通过自由、公正、定期的选举选择和更换执政者的权利。核心要素：多党竞争、普选权、秘密投票、权力和平交接。

The right of the people to choose and replace their rulers through free, fair, and regular elections. Core elements: multi-party competition, universal suffrage, secret ballot, peaceful transfer of power.

法治 Rule of Law
中共定义 · CCP

"中国特色社会主义法治"：法律体系在党的领导下运行。党指导立法、影响司法、不受司法审查约束。"依法治国"意味着通过法律来治理，而非法律约束统治者。

"Rule of law with Chinese characteristics": the legal system operates under Party leadership. The Party guides legislation, influences the judiciary, and is not subject to judicial review.

自由民主定义 · Liberal Democratic

没有人在法律之上，包括最高领导人。司法独立于行政权力；法律对所有人平等适用；政府行为可以在法庭上被质疑。关键：法治与法制完全不同。前者约束权力，后者只是用法律作为权力的工具。

Nobody is above the law, including the supreme leader. The judiciary is independent; laws apply equally to all. The critical distinction: rule OF law vs. rule BY law. The former constrains power; the latter merely uses law as power's instrument.

自由 Freedom
中共定义 · CCP

社会主义核心价值观之一，但被置于国家利益的框架内。自由必须"在法律范围内行使"（而法律由党制定）。信息自由被审查限制；集会需要批准；言论自由不包括批评党。

Listed as a "core socialist value," but situated within the framework of national interest. Freedom must be "exercised within the scope of law" (and the Party writes the law). Information is restricted by censorship; assembly requires approval; free speech does not include criticizing the Party.

自由民主定义 · Liberal Democratic

个人免受政府干预的权利。言论自由意味着可以公开批评总统而不会被逮捕。新闻自由意味着媒体可以报道政府的错误。这些自由可以受到有限的限制，但限制本身必须经受独立法院的审查。

The right of individuals to be free from government interference. Free speech means publicly criticizing the president without arrest. Free press means media can report government failures. These can be subject to limited restrictions, but restrictions must withstand review by independent courts.

人权 Human Rights
中共定义 · CCP

以"生存权和发展权"为首要人权。经济发展和脱贫是最大的人权成就。政治权利被置于经济发展之后。人权是国家主权范围内的事务，外部批评被拒绝为"干涉内政"。

"Rights to subsistence and development" are treated as the primary human rights. Political rights are subordinated. External criticism is "interference in internal affairs."

自由民主定义 · Liberal Democratic

人权是不可分割的：经济权利和政治权利同等重要，不可用前者替代后者。一个人吃饱饭但不能说话、不能投票、随时可以被消失，不算"享有人权"。人权是普世的，一个政府不能以"主权"为借口对本国人民犯下暴行。

Human rights are indivisible: economic and political rights are equally important, and one cannot substitute for the other. A person who is fed but cannot speak, cannot vote, and can be disappeared does not "enjoy human rights." Human rights are universal; a government cannot use "sovereignty" as an excuse for atrocities.

稳定 Stability
中共定义 · CCP

维护社会稳定是首要政治任务。"维稳"体系投入巨大资源压制异见、监控公民、控制信息流动。抗议和批评被视为对稳定的威胁。

"Maintaining stability" is the paramount political task. The system suppresses dissent, surveils citizens, and controls information. Protests and criticism are treated as threats to stability.

自由民主定义 · Liberal Democratic

真正的稳定来自于人们对制度的信任和参与，而非恐惧和沉默。抗议和异见不是不稳定，而是压力释放阀，让问题在变成危机之前得到解决。一个禁止批评的制度不是稳定的，它只是脆弱的。

True stability comes from trust and participation, not fear and silence. Protests and dissent are pressure valves that let problems be addressed before they become crises. A system that forbids criticism isn't stable; it is merely brittle.

制度、概念与现实

Institutions, Concepts, and Reality
权力分立与制衡

孟德斯鸠提出，权力必须用权力来制约。国家权力被分为三个独立的分支：立法（制定法律）、行政（执行法律）、司法（解释和应用法律）。

这三个分支相互制衡。总统不能控制法院；议会不能指挥军队；法官不能自己立法。这种设计故意使政府行动缓慢，因为缓慢比暴政更好。

黄宗羲三百年前就提出了类似的主张：宰相应当有实权制衡皇帝。权力分立不是西方的发明，而是对一个普遍问题的普遍回答：如何防止绝对权力的腐败。

在中国：所有权力集中在党手中。全国人大被称为"橡皮图章"；法院接受政法委指导；军队向党效忠而非向国家效忠。没有任何机构可以制约最高权力。而历史一再证明，不受约束的权力终将被滥用。

张又侠事件：不受制衡的后果

2026年1月，中央军委副主席张又侠被宣布接受调查。自2023年以来，中央军委七名成员中已有六人被免职或调查，火箭军领导层被整体清洗，数十名将军被撤职。这是文化大革命以来最大规模的军事领导层动荡。

官方说法是"反腐败"。但这些人都是习近平自己提拔的。如果他任命的人全都腐败了，这说明的是个人问题还是制度问题？在有制衡的制度中，军事领导人受到立法机构、独立法院和自由媒体的监督。在中国，一切在黑箱中进行。没有人知道真正的原因是反腐还是清洗。

独立司法

在自由民主制度中，法院独立于政府运作。法官不能因为做出政府不喜欢的裁决而被撤职。任何人，包括总统或总理，都可以被起诉。政府的行为可以被宣布违法或违宪。这不是对政府的敌意，而是对所有人（包括掌权者）适用同一套规则。

2024年，美国最高法院裁定特朗普总统不享有绝对刑事豁免权。坐在被告席上的是现任总统。做出裁决的法院中有他自己任命的法官。这在中国不可想象。

在中国：法院由政法委员会领导，该委员会是党的机关。法官的任命、晋升和薪酬都由党控制。在涉及政治的案件中，判决在审判之前就已经决定。2015年7月，当局在一次协调行动中拘留了300多名人权律师和活动人士（"709大抓捕"）。他们的"罪行"是在法律体系内为当事人辩护。当律师本身因为做自己的工作而被逮捕时，法治就已经不存在了。

新闻自由与第四权

独立媒体被视为"第四权力"，与立法、行政、司法并列。它的作用是监督政府、揭露腐败、向公众提供信息。

一个简单的测试：记者能否在不被逮捕的情况下报道领导人的丑闻？在民主国家，答案是可以。这恰恰是记者的工作。

在中国：所有主要媒体是党的"喉舌"（官方术语）。记者因报道真相而入狱。审查系统实时删除信息。防火长城阻断外部世界。当只能听到一个声音时，怎么知道那个声音在说真话？

言论自由的逻辑

约翰·斯图尔特·密尔提供了最有力的论证：

如果一个观点是正确的，压制它就剥夺了人们了解真相的机会。如果一个观点是错误的，公开辩论会揭示它的错误。如果一个观点部分正确、部分错误（这是最常见的情况），只有通过自由讨论才能区分。

密尔的论证指向一个令人不安的结论：审查几乎总是造成伤害，因为审查机构必须假定自己不会犯错。而认为自己不会犯错，本身就是一种危险的错误。

想一想新冠疫情初期：李文亮医生试图预警，被警方训诫"造谣"。如果他的声音没有被压制，疫情可能被更早控制。审查不仅侵犯权利，它可以致命。

如果政府的政策真的是最好的，为什么害怕公开讨论？一个真正经得起检验的主张，不需要审查来保护它。

当AI遇到不受约束的权力

人工智能常常被描述为中美之间的"军备竞赛"。这个框架对双方都有害，因为它把一个关乎所有人的问题变成了一场只关乎国家实力的较量。更诚实的问题是：AI应当为谁服务？在什么规则下运作？由谁来监督？

在新疆，AI驱动的面部识别系统专门识别维吾尔族面孔。监控摄像头追踪每个人的移动，算法在人们离开社区边界时自动报警。一家中国公司的系统明确标注：如果一个社区原本有一个维吾尔人，20天内出现了六个，立即向执法机关发出警报。

批评中国的AI监控并不意味着美国做得好。美国警察广泛使用面部识别技术，且识别错误率对少数族裔明显更高。区别在于：在美国，这些问题可以被公开报道、被法律挑战、被公众辩论。旧金山已立法禁止面部识别。在中国，一个揭露AI监控滥用的记者会被逮捕，而非获奖。

AI本身不是问题。问题是AI被谁控制，以及有没有任何力量可以制约这种控制。一个拥有世界上最先进AI技术的国家，如果其公民无法质疑这些技术的使用方式，那么这项技术的主要受益者不是人民，而是权力本身。

个人权利与少数群体保护

民主不仅仅是多数人说了算。它还意味着保护少数人免受多数人的暴政。

宪政民主的核心是：宪法规定了即使99%的人同意也不能被剥夺的权利。一个真正的民主制度不会因为多数人投票就允许迫害少数民族。

今天是多数，明天可能就是少数。保护少数人的权利就是保护每一个人的权利。这正是无知之幕的逻辑。

《促进民族团结进步法》

2026年3月，全国人大通过了这部法律，将于同年7月生效。它要求学龄前儿童学习普通话，要求少数民族文字在公共场合必须小于汉字，要求父母"教育引导未成年人热爱中国共产党"，并禁止向未成年人"灌输不利于民族团结进步的思想"。它还赋予国家对中国境外被认为"破坏民族团结"的个人和组织进行追诉的权力。

这部法律与中国宪法第四条直接矛盾，该条保障"各民族都有使用和发展自己的语言文字的自由"。它取代了1984年曾保障少数民族语言权利的《民族区域自治法》。一部以"团结"为名的法律，将同化写进了法律条文。

公民社会与独立工会

在政府和个人之间存在一个巨大的空间：公民社会。包括独立的工会、宗教团体、环保组织、慈善机构、学术机构等。它让人们在不通过政府的情况下组织起来，解决问题、表达利益、互相帮助。

在中国：独立公民社会组织被系统性地压制。NGO受严格监管；律师因维权而被"失踪"。香港曾是亚洲最自由的公民社会，2020年《国安法》实施后被迅速瓦解。新疆的大规模拘禁营和西藏持续的文化压制，展示了不受约束的权力对少数群体意味着什么。党试图充当所有中间组织的角色，但一个声称代表所有人的组织，实际上不对任何人负责。

最能说明问题的例子是工会。中国拥有世界上最庞大的制造业劳动力，但独立工会被完全禁止。唯一合法的工会（中华全国总工会）是党的机构，不代表工人与雇主谈判，而是协调工人服从党的政策。当工人试图自行组织时，组织者会被拘留。数亿人每天工作，却没有任何独立组织代表自己的利益。

竞争性选举与权力交接

自由民主不是一种信仰，它是一套制度。这些制度基于一个清醒的认识：所有人都可能腐败，所有权力都可能被滥用。因此，系统必须假定最坏的情况，并通过制度来约束它。

不同的政党和候选人提出不同的政策，公民通过投票选择。失败的一方和平交出权力，成为反对派，准备下次选举。这个机制解决了人类政治中最古老的问题：如何在不流血的情况下更换糟糕的领导人。

回顾中国历史，几乎每一次权力交接都伴随暴力。即使在中共内部，权力交接也充满清洗和阴谋。取消国家主席任期限制进一步消除了和平交接的保障。

选举不是完美的，民选领导人也可能无能或腐败。但选举提供了一个不可替代的功能：一个和平淘汰坏领导人的机制。没有这个机制，只能等待。等待好皇帝的出现，或者等待坏皇帝的死亡。

宪政主义

宪政意味着政府的权力受到宪法的严格限制，且这些限制是可执行的。存在独立的法院，可以宣布政府的行为违宪。

中国也有宪法。中国宪法甚至规定了言论自由、新闻自由、集会自由和宗教信仰自由。问题在于：这些条款从未被执行。没有任何机构可以追究政府违宪的责任。

2013年后，"宪政"一词被列为媒体敏感词。一个政府为什么会害怕自己宪法中使用的概念？这个问题本身就值得深思。

Separation of Powers and Checks and Balances

Montesquieu argued that power must check power. State power is divided into three independent branches: legislative, executive, and judicial.

These branches check each other. The president cannot control courts; parliament cannot command the military; judges cannot make laws. This design intentionally makes government slow, because slow is better than tyrannical.

Huang Zongxi proposed a similar idea three centuries ago: the prime minister should have real power to check the emperor. Separation of powers is not a Western invention but a universal answer to a universal problem: how to prevent the corruption of absolute power.

In China: All power is concentrated in the Party. The NPC is called a "rubber stamp"; courts take direction from the Political and Legal Affairs Commission; the military swears loyalty to the Party, not the nation. No institution can constrain supreme power, and history repeatedly proves that unchecked power will eventually be abused.

The Zhang Youxia Purge: The Consequences of No Checks

In January 2026, CMC Vice Chairman Zhang Youxia was placed under investigation. Since 2023, six of seven CMC members have been removed. The Rocket Force leadership was purged entirely. This is the most significant military upheaval since the Cultural Revolution.

The official explanation is "anti-corruption." But these were all people Xi himself promoted. If everyone he appointed is corrupt, does that indict the individuals or the system? In a system with checks, military leaders face oversight from legislatures, courts, and free media. In China, everything happens in a black box. No one knows the real reasons.

An Independent Judiciary

In a liberal democracy, courts operate independently from the government. Judges cannot be removed for making rulings the government dislikes. Anyone, including the president or prime minister, can be prosecuted. Government actions can be declared illegal or unconstitutional. This is not hostility toward government; it is the same rules applying to everyone, including those in power.

In 2024, the US Supreme Court ruled that President Trump does not enjoy absolute criminal immunity. The sitting president was in the dock. The court that ruled included judges he himself appointed. This is unimaginable in China.

In China: Courts are led by the Political and Legal Affairs Commission, a Party organ. Judges' appointments, promotions, and salaries are controlled by the Party. In politically sensitive cases, verdicts are decided before trials begin. In July 2015, authorities detained over 300 human rights lawyers and activists in a single coordinated sweep (the "709 Crackdown"). Their "crime" was defending clients within the legal system. When lawyers themselves are arrested for doing their jobs, the rule of law has ceased to exist.

Freedom of the Press and the Fourth Estate

Independent media is considered the "fourth estate," alongside the legislature, executive, and judiciary. Its role: hold government accountable, expose corruption, inform the public.

A simple test: can a journalist report on a leader's scandal without being arrested? In democracies, yes. That is precisely the journalist's job.

In China: All major media are the Party's "mouthpiece" (the official term). Journalists are imprisoned for reporting truth. Censorship deletes information in real time. The Great Firewall blocks the outside world. When only one voice can be heard, how can anyone know that voice is telling the truth?

The Logic of Free Speech

John Stuart Mill provided the most powerful argument:

If an opinion is correct, suppressing it robs people of the truth. If wrong, open debate reveals its errors. If partly right and partly wrong (the most common case), only free discussion can distinguish which parts are which.

Mill's argument points to an uncomfortable conclusion: censorship almost always causes harm, because the censoring authority must assume its own infallibility. And believing oneself infallible is itself a dangerous error.

Consider the early days of COVID-19: Dr. Li Wenliang tried to sound the alarm and was reprimanded by police for "rumor-mongering." If his voice had not been suppressed, the outbreak might have been contained earlier. Censorship doesn't just violate rights; it can be lethal.

If the government's policies are truly the best, why fear open discussion? A claim that truly withstands scrutiny does not need censorship to protect it.

When AI Meets Unchecked Power

Artificial intelligence is often described as an "arms race" between China and the United States. This framing is harmful to both sides, because it turns a question that affects everyone into a contest that serves only national power. The more honest questions are: Who should AI serve? Under what rules? Overseen by whom?

In Xinjiang, AI-powered facial recognition systems are specifically trained to identify Uyghur faces. Surveillance cameras track everyone's movements; algorithms alert authorities when people cross neighborhood boundaries. One Chinese company's system explicitly states: if a neighborhood originally had one Uyghur resident and six appear within 20 days, it immediately alerts law enforcement.

Criticizing China's AI surveillance does not mean the US is doing well. American police widely use facial recognition with higher error rates for minorities. The difference: in the US, these problems can be publicly reported, legally challenged, and publicly debated. San Francisco has legislated a ban. In China, a journalist exposing AI surveillance abuse would be arrested, not awarded.

AI itself is not the problem. The problem is who controls AI, and whether any force exists to constrain that control. A country with the world's most advanced AI, whose citizens cannot question how that technology is used, has built a tool that serves power, not people.

Individual Rights and Protection of Minorities

Democracy is not simply majority rule. It also means protecting minorities from the tyranny of the majority.

The core of constitutional democracy: the constitution establishes rights that cannot be stripped away even if 99% agree. A genuine democratic system would not allow persecution of an ethnic minority because a majority voted for it.

Today's majority is tomorrow's minority. Protecting minority rights protects everyone's rights. This is the logic of the veil of ignorance.

The Law on Promoting Ethnic Unity and Progress

In March 2026, the NPC passed this law, effective July 2026. It requires preschool children to learn Mandarin, requires minority scripts to be displayed smaller than Chinese characters in public, requires parents to "educate minors to love the Chinese Communist Party," and prohibits "instilling in minors ideas detrimental to ethnic unity." It also asserts jurisdiction over individuals and organizations outside China deemed to "undermine ethnic unity."

This law directly contradicts Article 4 of China's own constitution, which guarantees all ethnic groups "the freedom to use and develop their own spoken and written languages." It replaces the 1984 Regional Autonomy Law that had guaranteed minority language rights. A law named "promoting unity" writes assimilation into statute.

Civil Society and Independent Unions

Between the government and the individual exists a vast space: civil society. This includes independent unions, religious groups, environmental organizations, charities, and academic institutions. It allows people to organize without going through the government.

In China: Independent civil society is systematically suppressed. NGOs face strict regulation; lawyers are "disappeared." Hong Kong once had Asia's freest civil society; after the 2020 National Security Law, it was rapidly dismantled. The mass internment camps in Xinjiang and ongoing cultural repression in Tibet show what unchecked power means for minorities. The Party fills all intermediary roles, but an organization claiming to represent everyone is accountable to no one.

The most telling example is labor unions. China has the world's largest manufacturing workforce, but independent unions are banned. The sole legal union is a Party institution that coordinates compliance, not collective bargaining. When workers try to organize independently, organizers are detained. Hundreds of millions work daily with no independent organization to represent their interests.

Competitive Elections and Transfer of Power

Liberal democracy is not a faith; it is a set of institutions. These are designed from a clear-eyed recognition: all people can be corrupted, and all power can be abused. Therefore, the system must assume the worst and constrain it through institutions.

Different parties and candidates propose different policies; citizens choose through voting. The losing side peacefully surrenders power, becomes the opposition, and prepares for the next election. This mechanism solves the oldest problem in politics: how to remove bad leaders without bloodshed.

In Chinese history, nearly every power transition involved violence. Even within the CCP, transitions have been marked by purges and conspiracies. Abolishing presidential term limits further eliminated guarantees of peaceful transition.

Elections are not perfect; elected leaders can be incompetent or corrupt. But elections provide an irreplaceable function: a mechanism for peacefully removing bad leaders. Without it, one can only wait. Wait for a good emperor to appear, or for a bad emperor to die.

Constitutionalism

Constitutionalism means government power is strictly limited by a constitution, and those limits are enforceable. An independent court exists that can declare government actions unconstitutional.

China also has a constitution. It even guarantees freedom of speech, press, assembly, and religious belief. The problem: these provisions have never been enforced. No institution can hold the government accountable for violations.

After 2013, the word "constitutionalism" was added to the list of censored media terms. Why would a government fear a concept contained in its own constitution? The question itself is worth sitting with.

特朗普、反驳与反对犬儒

Trump, Objections, and Against Cynicism
特朗普不是意外，而是一种古老模式的回归

威权主义不是某个文化或某个时代的特产。它是一种永恒的人类倾向：对强人的渴望、对简单答案的偏好、对"敌人"的需要。每一个社会都会产生试图集中权力的领导人。美国也不例外。

美国历史上充满了威权的冲动：奴隶制、对原住民的灭绝、麦卡锡时代的政治迫害、尼克松的滥权。特朗普不是美国的例外，而是这条线索的最新一环。他攻击媒体、施压司法、质疑选举结果、煽动支持者冲击国会。这些不是民主制度的正常运作，而是对民主制度的攻击。

这是特朗普是中国官方媒体最喜欢的例子的原因："看，这就是民主的结果。"这个批评有一定的道理，应当诚实地面对。

但请注意接下来发生了什么：

法院（包括他自己任命的法官）驳回了他关于选举舞弊的诉讼。媒体持续报道和批评他，没有一家报纸因此被关闭。数百万人上街抗议，没有人被坦克碾压。国会对他进行了弹劾审判。他在2020年输掉了选举，权力和平交接了。即使他在2024年重新执政，反对派仍然存在，法院仍然可以否决他的命令，媒体仍然可以批评他，人民仍然可以在下次选举中做出不同的选择。

这就是制度的意义。制度不能阻止坏领导人出现（没有任何制度能做到这一点）。制度的作用是：当坏领导人出现时，限制他的破坏力，并提供和平更换他的途径。

现在做一个对比：当毛泽东发动大跃进导致数千万人饿死时，有没有法院可以阻止他？有没有媒体可以报道真相？有没有选举可以更换他？答案全部是"没有"。灾难持续了三年，直到他自愿改变方向。这就是没有制度制衡的代价。

问题不是"民主能否产生坏领导人"（当然能），问题是：当坏领导人出现时，你有什么办法？

"民主导致混乱"。世界上最稳定、最繁荣的国家几乎都是民主国家：日本、韩国、德国、加拿大、北欧国家。而20世纪最大的人为灾难（大跃进饥荒，约三千万人死亡；文化大革命）恰恰发生在一党专政之下，且正因为没有制衡机制才持续了那么久。

民主有时显得混乱：辩论、抗议、政治僵局。但这种"混乱"是社会自我纠错的过程。一个不允许混乱的制度把问题隐藏起来，直到它们以更大的规模爆发。地震的比喻依然适用：是更愿意住在经常有小地震释放压力的地方，还是一个几十年没有地震然后突然来一次9级大地震的地方？

明尼阿波利斯与"指定居所监视居住"。2026年1月，美国联邦移民执法人员在明尼阿波利斯射杀了两名美国公民：Renee Good和Alex Pretti。这是严重的国家暴力事件。但请注意之后发生了什么。

射杀事件被独立媒体实时报道，旁观者视频自由传播。50,000人在零下20华氏度中上街抗议，700多家企业罢工，100多名宗教领袖在机场进行公民不服从并被捕。联邦法官裁定执法机构违反了至少96项法院命令。司法部启动民权调查。这一切公开、可追踪、可问责。运动由普通社区成员自发组织，而非任何政党发起。

现在做一个对比。在中国，当国家机关杀害或"消失"公民时，会发生什么？中国有一种叫做"指定居所监视居住"的制度。在这个制度下，警方可以将任何人关押在秘密地点长达六个月，不通知家属、不允许见律师、不公开关押地点。联合国专家已将其定性为"强迫失踪的一种形式"。据民间组织估计，2013年至2021年间有约85,000人经历了这一制度。人权律师高智晟自2017年起失踪至今，他的家人不知道他是死是活。

没有旁观者视频会流传。没有媒体会报道。没有人敢走上街头。没有法院可以裁定政府违法。没有调查。没有问责。受害者消失了，就好像他们从未存在过。

这就是有制度制衡和没有制度制衡的区别。不是民主制度下不会发生暴行，而是暴行发生后，存在暴露它、反抗它、追究它的空间。

"西方民主不适合中国"：台湾的反驳。对这个论点最有力的回应只需要两个字：台湾。

台湾与大陆共享相同的语言、相同的文字、相同的文化传统、相同的历史根源。台湾同样经历过威权统治：国民党在台湾实行了近四十年的戒严。如果"中国文化不适合民主"的论点成立，台湾应该是最好的证据。

但事实恰恰相反。台湾在1980年代末开始民主转型，1996年举行了第一次总统直选，此后实现了多次和平的政党轮替。今天，台湾是亚洲最活跃的民主政体之一：选举是竞争性的，媒体是自由的，司法是独立的，公民社会蓬勃发展，网络没有审查。

台湾也没有因此崩溃。台湾是全球半导体产业的核心，拥有发达的经济、优质的医疗和教育体系、极低的犯罪率和高度的社会凝聚力。民主不但没有阻碍台湾的发展，反而与繁荣和稳定共存。

如果两千三百万中国人可以在民主制度下自由投票、自由表达、自由生活，为什么十四亿中国人不能？"不适合中国"的论点隐含着一个问题：它到底是在保护中国文化，还是在低估中国人民？"民主不适合中国"不是传统，它是掌权之后的发明。

民主不是一个结果，它是一个过程。中共经常把民主等同于特定的政策结果："人民生活改善了，所以我们有民主。"但民主不是一个结果，而是一个过程：一个让人民参与决策、纠正错误、更换领导人的过程。

一个仁慈的独裁者可能做出好的决策。但这依赖于独裁者碰巧是仁慈的。下一个呢？再下一个呢？民主的优势不是保证最好的结果，而是保证最坏的结果不会太坏，而且可以被纠正。

用一个比喻：民主不是选出最好的司机，而是确保车上有方向盘、有刹车，而且乘客可以要求停车。

"中国的发展证明了体制的优越性"。中国的经济增长确实了不起。但增长最快的时期恰恰是党放松控制的时期。韩国和台湾在威权时期也高速增长，随后成功转型为民主。经济发展不要求一党专政永远持续。

一个制度的好坏不能只用GDP衡量。增长的代价是什么？增长的成果由谁分享？当增长放缓时，一个没有选举合法性的政府靠什么维持权力？

战争与问责。民主制度会发动不正义的战争吗？当然会。美国在越南、伊拉克和阿富汗的战争都是毫无疑问的例子。民主并不阻止政府做出灾难性的决定。

但在每一个案例中，公众的反对最终迫使政府改变方向。越战的反战运动迫使约翰逊放弃连任；伊拉克战争的虚假理由被自由媒体揭露；阿富汗二十年后公众不再支持，撤军发生。这个过程缓慢、痛苦、代价高昂，但纠错最终发生了，因为制度中存在纠错机制：自由媒体、合法抗议、公开批评、选票更换领导人。

在威权制度中，这个纠错过程不存在。领导人不需要向任何人解释战争的理由。媒体不会报道真实的伤亡数字。反战抗议会被镇压。没有选举来更换做出灾难性决策的领导人。战争可以在沉默中持续下去，因为承受代价的人民没有任何手段来阻止它。

这与中国直接相关。如果中国的领导层决定对台湾采取军事行动，谁来阻止他们？军队向党效忠，不向人民效忠。媒体是党的喉舌。没有反对党，没有独立法院，没有合法的抗议途径。做出这个决定的人不需要承担任何政治后果，而这个决定的代价将由无数普通人承受。

犬儒主义是威权的最佳盟友

也许读到这里，最自然的反应是："所有政府都一样腐败。所有制度都是骗人的。中国和美国没什么区别，只是方式不同而已。"这正是威权政府最希望人们相信的。

独裁政权最有效的武器不是宣传（宣传的效果有限，因为大多数人不会真正相信它）。最有效的武器是犬儒主义：让人们相信没有什么是真的，没有什么可以改变，所有声称在乎人民的人其实都在骗人。

当犬儒占据上风时，人们就放弃了。不再追问、不再期待、不再行动。接受现状，不是因为认为它是好的，而是因为相信没有什么更好的选择。

犬儒主义把自己伪装成智慧："我看穿了一切。"但值得问一问：如果所有制度真的一样差，为什么有些国家的人可以公开骂总统而另一些国家的人因此入狱？如果真的没有区别，为什么审查制度如此努力地阻止人们进行这种比较？

利害攸关

如果美国未能捍卫自己的民主价值，如果它滑向寡头政治和制度侵蚀，那么世界上最强大的民主范例将失去说服力。威权政府将有更大的空间说："看，民主不管用。"

如果中国继续扩展其威权模式（出口监控技术、输出审查系统、用经济压力让其他国家沉默），那么全世界的自由空间都会缩小。这不是假设：它已经在发生。

但这个故事还有另一面。中国有14亿聪明、勤劳、富有创造力的人。他们在一个极其困难的环境中展现了非凡的韧性和智慧。如果有一天，这些人能够生活在一个保护他们尊严、倾听他们声音、允许他们自由思考的制度下，不仅中国会更好，世界也会更好。

这不是中国对西方的竞争。这是所有地方的人民对所有地方的不受约束的权力的共同抗争。每一个渴望自由表达、公正对待和有尊严生活的人，无论在北京还是华盛顿，都面对着同一个根本问题：如何确保掌权者为人民服务，而不是人民为掌权者服务。

自由地分享。自由地思考。

Trump Is Not an Accident, but the Return of an Ancient Pattern

Authoritarianism is not the product of any particular culture or era. It is a permanent human tendency: the longing for a strongman, the preference for simple answers, the need for an "enemy." Every society produces leaders who try to concentrate power. The United States is no exception.

American history is full of authoritarian impulses: slavery, the genocide of indigenous peoples, McCarthyist political persecution, Nixon's abuses of power. Trump is not an aberration in American history but the latest link in this chain. He attacks the media, pressures the judiciary, disputes election results, and incited supporters to storm the Capitol. These are not the normal functioning of democratic institutions; they are attacks on them.

This is why Trump is Chinese state media's favorite example: "See, this is what democracy produces." The criticism has some validity, and it should be faced honestly.

But note what happened next:

Courts (including judges he himself appointed) rejected his fraud claims. Media continued reporting and criticizing him; no newspaper was shut down. Millions protested; no one was crushed by tanks. Congress held impeachment proceedings. He lost in 2020, and power was peacefully transferred. Even after returning to office in 2024, the opposition still exists, courts can still overturn his orders, media can still criticize him, and people can still make a different choice next time.

This is what institutions are for. Institutions cannot prevent bad leaders from appearing (no system can). What institutions do: when bad leaders appear, they limit the damage and provide a peaceful path to replacement.

Now consider a contrast: when Mao launched the Great Leap Forward and tens of millions starved, were there courts to stop him? Media to report the truth? Elections to replace him? The answer to all is "no." The disaster continued for three years until he voluntarily changed course. That is the cost of having no institutional checks.

The question is not "can democracy produce bad leaders?" (of course it can). The question is: when a bad leader appears, what can you do about it?

"Democracy Leads to Chaos." The most stable and prosperous countries are almost all democracies: Japan, South Korea, Germany, Canada, the Nordic countries. The greatest man-made disasters of the 20th century (the Great Leap Forward famine, approximately 30 million dead; the Cultural Revolution) happened under one-party rule, and continued precisely because there were no checks.

Democracy can look messy: debates, protests, gridlock. But this "mess" is society self-correcting. A system that doesn't allow messiness hides problems until they erupt on a larger scale. The earthquake analogy holds: is it better to live where frequent small quakes release pressure, or where decades of silence precede a magnitude-9 quake?

Minneapolis and "Residential Surveillance at a Designated Location." In January 2026, US federal immigration agents shot and killed two American citizens in Minneapolis: Renee Good and Alex Pretti. These were serious acts of state violence. But note what happened next.

The killings were reported in real time; bystander video circulated freely. 50,000 marched in minus 20 Fahrenheit cold, 700 plus businesses shut down, 100 plus clergy arrested in civil disobedience. A federal judge found agents violated at least 96 court orders. The DOJ opened a civil rights investigation. All public, traceable, accountable. The movement was organized by ordinary community members, not any political party.

Now consider the contrast. In China, when state agents kill or "disappear" citizens, what happens? China has a system called "Residential Surveillance at a Designated Location" (RSDL). Under it, police can hold anyone in a secret location for up to six months without notifying family, without access to a lawyer, without disclosing where they are held. UN experts have classified this as "a form of enforced disappearance." Civil society organizations estimate approximately 85,000 people were subjected to RSDL between 2013 and 2021. Human rights lawyer Gao Zhisheng has been missing since 2017; his family does not know if he is alive.

No bystander video circulates. No media reports. No one dares take to the streets. No court can rule the government acted illegally. No investigation. No accountability. The victims disappear as if they never existed.

This is the difference between having institutional checks and not having them. It is not that atrocities cannot happen in a democracy, but that when they do, there is space to expose them, resist them, and hold those responsible to account.

"Western Democracy Doesn't Suit China": Taiwan Says Otherwise. The most powerful response to this argument requires only one word: Taiwan.

Taiwan shares the same language, the same script, the same cultural traditions, and the same historical roots as the mainland. Taiwan also experienced authoritarian rule: the KMT imposed nearly four decades of martial law. If the argument that "Chinese culture is incompatible with democracy" held, Taiwan should be the strongest evidence for it.

The reality is the opposite. Taiwan began its democratic transition in the late 1980s, held its first direct presidential election in 1996, and has since achieved multiple peaceful transfers of power between parties. Today, Taiwan is one of Asia's most vibrant democracies: elections are competitive, media is free, the judiciary is independent, civil society flourishes, and the internet is uncensored.

Taiwan has not collapsed as a result. Taiwan sits at the heart of the global semiconductor industry, with an advanced economy, excellent healthcare and education systems, extremely low crime rates, and high social cohesion. Democracy has not hindered Taiwan's development; it coexists with prosperity and stability.

If 23 million Chinese people can freely vote, speak, and live under democracy, why can't 1.4 billion? The "doesn't suit China" argument raises a question: is it protecting Chinese culture, or underestimating the Chinese people? "Democracy doesn't suit China" is not tradition; it is a post-power invention.

Democracy Is Not an Outcome; It Is a Process. The CCP often equates democracy with specific policy outcomes: "people's lives improved, so we have democracy." But democracy is not an outcome; it is a process: one that lets people participate in decisions, correct mistakes, and replace leaders.

A benevolent dictator might make good decisions. But this depends on the dictator happening to be benevolent. What about the next one? And the one after that? Democracy's advantage is not guaranteeing the best outcomes, but ensuring the worst outcomes aren't too bad, and can be corrected.

An analogy: democracy is not about electing the best driver, but about ensuring the car has a steering wheel, brakes, and passengers who can demand to stop.

"China's Development Proves the System's Superiority." China's economic growth is remarkable. But the fastest growth came when the Party loosened control. South Korea and Taiwan also grew rapidly under authoritarianism, then transitioned to democracy. Development doesn't require one-party rule forever.

A system's quality cannot be measured by GDP alone. What is the cost of growth? Who shares its gains? When growth slows, what sustains a government with no electoral legitimacy?

War and Accountability. Do democracies start unjust wars? Of course. The US wars in Vietnam, Iraq, and Afghanistan are unambiguous examples. Democracy does not prevent governments from making catastrophic decisions.

But in every case, public opposition eventually forced change. Vietnam's anti-war movement forced Johnson to abandon reelection; Iraq's false pretenses were exposed by free media; Afghanistan saw withdrawal after public support collapsed. The process is slow, painful, and costly, but correction happens because the system contains mechanisms for it: free media, legal protest, open criticism, and the ballot box.

In an authoritarian system, this corrective process does not exist. Leaders do not need to explain their reasons for war to anyone. Media will not report true casualty figures. Anti-war protests will be suppressed. No election exists to replace leaders who make catastrophic decisions. A war can continue in silence because the people who bear its costs have no means to stop it.

This is directly relevant to China. If China's leadership decided to take military action against Taiwan, who would stop them? The military swears loyalty to the Party, not the people. Media is the Party's mouthpiece. There is no opposition party, no independent court, no legal avenue for protest. The people making that decision would bear no political consequences, while countless ordinary people would bear the costs.

Cynicism Is Authoritarianism's Best Ally

Perhaps the most natural reaction at this point is: "All governments are equally corrupt. All systems are frauds. China and America are no different, just different styles." This is exactly what authoritarian governments most want people to believe.

An authoritarian regime's most effective weapon is not propaganda (propaganda has limited effect because most people don't truly believe it). The most effective weapon is cynicism: making people believe nothing is true, nothing can change, and everyone who claims to care about the people is lying.

When cynicism prevails, people give up. They stop questioning, stop expecting, stop acting. They accept the status quo, not because they think it's good, but because they believe nothing better is possible.

Cynicism disguises itself as wisdom: "I see through everything." But it is worth asking: if all systems are truly equally bad, why can people in some countries publicly curse their president while in others they are imprisoned for it? If there really is no difference, why does the censorship system work so hard to prevent people from making the comparison?

What Is at Stake

If America fails to uphold its democratic values, if it slides toward oligarchy and institutional erosion, the world's most powerful democratic example loses its persuasive force. Authoritarian governments gain more room to say: "See, democracy doesn't work."

If China continues to expand its authoritarian model (exporting surveillance technology, censorship systems, using economic pressure to silence other nations), free space around the world shrinks. This is not hypothetical: it is already happening.

But there is another side to this story. China has 1.4 billion intelligent, hardworking, creative people. They have shown extraordinary resilience and wisdom under extremely difficult conditions. If one day these people can live under a system that protects their dignity, listens to their voices, and allows them to think freely, not only China would be better, but the world would be better.

This is not a competition between China and the West. It is a shared struggle of people everywhere against unchecked power everywhere. Everyone who desires free expression, fair treatment, and a dignified life, whether in Beijing or Washington, faces the same fundamental question: how to ensure those in power serve the people, rather than the people serving those in power.
